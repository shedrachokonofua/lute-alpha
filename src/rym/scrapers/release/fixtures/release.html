Hurricanslash Aug 04 2020
At this point it becomes increasingly difficult for me to write about MIKE and his music, and that is not because of a dip in quality or anything, but more because of how much he basically uses his music as diary-entries, depicting his struggles and emotional journey since the last project, all the while experimenting with his musical style. I do not want to give a rating to someones honest expression of his emotions, because... you know, that's mighty inconsiderate. Especially when the album is about trying to completely accept the loss of ones own mother and pulling positives out of a negative situation, all the while feeling so overwhelmed that, yes, you perceive the weight of the whole world on your shoulders. It's heartbreaking, emotional, but optimistic, and I am glad to see MIKE going in a more positive direction once more and generally feel better. The only thing I really feel comfortable rating is the music itself, which is great, as always. Yes, on this project some beats don't hit as much as previous offerings, but the lax, almost mockingly easygoing atmosphere the smooth jazz, funk and soul-beats bring to the table is a distinctly less glitchy affair, feeling more like the world just keeps on rushing by you, unaware of what you are going through. It's probably his most accessible production yet next to MGBYH, and if you are looking to get into his music, this is as good a time as ever. I just hope that MIKE is doing good.

FEEBLY FLIPPING THROUGH A FAMILY SCRAPBOOK
As someone whose been following MIKE's career since 2017's fantastic May God Bless Your Hustle, the current state of tautology he's found himself in is nothing short of disappointing. While MIKE may have originated the sound, promoted handedly by Standing On The Corner's jazzier parallels, Earl Sweatshirt's prominent Some Rap Songs essentially cornered MIKE into this syrupy, stuck-in-the-mud style of Lo-Fi Hip-Hop if he's to maintain relevance. And so he has. Weight Of The World, like last year's Tears Of Joy, finds MIKE circling around the drain of indecisiveness, reflecting on past transgressions through a bleary, heavily-damaged viewfinder. The songs are nothing more than snippets, like diary entries for unexceptional days or sketchbook drawings soon abandoned. At this point, with his cadence, tone, and lyrics calibrated to precision, MIKE's ensemble of production units is what keeps his potential afloat. Tracks like 'coat of many colors' and 'what's home 1/2' bear the weight with faded textures and lost generations of Soul samples while MIKE dawdles with his muzzled annunciation and disinterested charisma.

As far as comparisons to his past work goes, Weight Of The World falls somewhere in between the 2018 tandem of Black Soap and Renaissance Man. Dreary, confused, content clutching to a niche aesthetic. This is no War In My Pen, that's for sure. 'Weight Of The Word*' and 'Allstar' are the only above-average cuts on here.

ajcollins15 Jun 29 2020
Underground hip-hop, a genre that will pull you in and keep you there. MIKE is on his sixth album, and he is fairly consistent with one album per year. However, Mike has always been an ambiguous figure for me musically. Sometimes he comes through with powerful lyrics, soothing instrumentals, and odd beats. Plus, he has always been lower on my abstract hip-hop bar than others like Earl Sweatshirt and MAVI. However, on his most recent record, he comes through with some of his most powerful lyrics and best instrumentals to date. So many great samples come through on this record. However, there is still plenty of WTF happened on this record which has always plagued his music for me. Especially on several tracks that have no sense of direction (which is weird to say because the appeal of these records is because they are so one-note). In conclusion, this is the best album in the genre to date, but it still leaves me underwhelmed and makes me want to listen to others like him that have a better understanding of instrumentals.

Regardless of whether or not you find this style of rap endearing, MIKE is undoubtedly one of the leading creative visionaries in underground rap today. He’s not the first rapper to make hazy, lo-fi, soul-inflected inner-struggle raps. But he & the sLUms collective have taken this sound and elevated it to new heights, making it one of the forefront styles in the underground scene.

If ‘Tears of Joy’ was MIKE’s initial jumbled thought process in the wake of his mother’s passing, than ‘Weight of the World’ is MIKE after he’s taken some time to gather his thoughts on the whole situation, and thus he’s able to deliver well-coherent thoughts on the process of life after tragedy. Don’t be mistaken, he’s still very much hurting and reeling from the loss of his mother, but as a whole ‘Weight of the World’ does a better job at being a tribute and a soulful ode to his mother than ‘Tears of Joy’ did, which is probably why this is MIKE’s first proper follow-up album to 2018’s ‘Renaissance Man’.

As for the content of the album itself, although this is all still very much experimental and unorthodox, ‘Weight of the World’ might be MIKE’s most accessible work to date. Songs like “Get Rich Quick Scheme” & “Weight of the Word*” have some of MIKE’s most immediate production yet, and the second half of the latter song is the rare occasion where you can actually hear MIKE’s rapping loud & clear. He produced most of the album himself under his signature pseudonym DJ Blackpower, where he uses a lot of soul-sampled loops that are subtle, similar to producers like J Dilla or Knxwledge. Other songs on the album, like the long-awaited Earl Sweatshirt collaboration “Allstar” & intro track “Love Supremacy” deliver psychedelic soundscapes, and that especially rings true for “Coat of Many Colors”, a song who’s production is the equivalent of laying in the clouds marveling at the beauty around you. And then you have songs like “Alert*” & “222”, both of which are infused with lowkey piano lounge jazz. The one constant that remains in the production of ‘Weight of the World’ is that it’s all absolutely stunning and full of catharsis, and I can comfortably say it’s the best production I’ve heard on a rap album this year so far.

It goes without saying that MIKE’s raps are very personal, he’s always been one to confront his internal self when it comes to his rapping. He doesn’t try to be clever or witty with his penmanship, his raps are straightforward and blunt, they’re meant to be easy to understand because his words hit harder when you know what he’s talking about. That’s pretty much the whole style of rapping that he & his fellow peers have birthed. One of the largest criticisms that MIKE gets is that his rapping is lazy, which couldn’t be further from the truth. If anything, MIKE wears his heart on his sleeve every time he raps. He’s very firmly a substance over style kinda guy, not trying to rap flashy rhymes, but instead rapping lines that can hold serious emotional weight.

It’s precisely why the largely grief-stricken ‘Weight of the World’ is such a relatable album, especially nowadays where it feels like the world is genuinely being weighed down by all of the bullshit happening within it. For anyone who’s gone through or suffered a loss, or to anyone who’s just flat-out depressed, this is an album that they can find familiarity with. MIKE has created a piece of work that displays and showcases the internal feelings a lot of people are currently going through right now, and a lot of these people such as myself can find comfort in an album like this. And in that sense, it’s easy to make the argument that ‘Weight of the World’ is MIKE’s most essential album yet as his young & promising career continues to progress towards something even greater.

There was quite a lot of hype surrounding this album. It had a pretty high score on RYM, friends were excited for it and critics and fans alike were praising it. I think that all these things only made me more disappointed when I listened to Weight of the World.

With this record, I think MIKE was going for a laid-back, relaxed feel to the music. Instead, it comes across as boring; and, to some extent, lazy. His monotone, deadpan delivery makes him sound like he's bored, like he'd be anywhere else. This is not always bad, and this style of rapping can work in certain contexts. However, it doesn't here. This is mainly because of the lyrics, which tackle very personal topics. These lyrics are raw, and his mumbling, droning voice does not really match that well

The instrumentals are pretty good, and I like the vaporwave-y style, but they get pretty repetitive. The lo-fi production doesn't really sit well with me, even though I know that's what MIKE was going for in the first place.

Overall, this just wasn't for me. It's definitely good, and I think I might need to revisit it in the future, but I don't see coming back to it (willingly, at least) in the near future. It does have some great highlights, but they're too infrequent to make me rate this any higher than I have. Maybe I'm missing something, because everyone seems to love it.

6/10
